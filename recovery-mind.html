Recovery Tools & Coping Strategies

Practical tools to help you manage cravings, handle stress, and build a healthy life.

üõë HALT Check-In

Before making any major decision or when feeling triggered, ask yourself:

  • H - Am I Hungry?
  • A - Am I Angry?
  • L - Am I Lonely?
  • T - Am I Tired?

These states make you vulnerable to poor decisions. Address the need first.

üßò Grounding Exercise (5-4-3-2-1)

When cravings hit or anxiety rises, use your senses:

  • 5 things you can see
  • 4 things you can touch
  • 3 things you can hear
  • 2 things you can smell
  • 1 thing you can taste

This brings you back to the present moment and out of your head.

üí≠ Thought Stopping Technique

When obsessive thoughts about using appear:

  1. Recognize the thought immediately
  2. Say "STOP" firmly (out loud or in your mind)
  3. Replace it with a positive affirmation: "I am choosing recovery" or "This feeling will pass"
  4. Engage in a distracting activity immediately (exercise, reading, talking)

üìù Decision Matrix

Before making risky choices, write out:

  • Short-term consequences of using vs. not using
  • Long-term consequences of using vs. not using
  • Who will be affected by my choice
  • What my future self would want me to do

üéØ Goal Setting Framework

Set SMART goals for recovery:

  • Specific - Clear and well-defined
  • Measurable - Track your progress
  • Achievable - Realistic given your situation
  • Relevant - Matters to your recovery
  • Time-bound - Has a deadline

Example: "I will complete all 6 lessons in this program within 2 weeks and journal 3 times per week."

Understanding Criminal Thinking Patterns

Addiction often goes hand-in-hand with distorted thinking patterns that led to criminal behavior. Recognizing these patterns is the first step to changing them.

Common Criminal Thinking Errors:

1. Victim Stance

"Everyone is against me. The system is unfair. I had no choice."

Reality Check: While life can be unfair, you are responsible for your choices. Taking ownership is the path to freedom.

2. Entitlement

"I deserve this. They have it, why shouldn't I? Rules don't apply to me."

Reality Check: Nobody owes you anything. Respect for others and their property is foundational to recovery.

3. Power & Control

"I need to dominate. Weakness is unacceptable. Nobody tells me what to do."

Reality Check: True strength is in humility, accepting help, and controlling yourself, not others.

4. Justification & Minimizing

"It's not that bad. Everyone does it. They deserved it. I needed the money."

Reality Check: Making excuses keeps you stuck. Face the truth about your actions and their impact.

5. Inability to Delay Gratification

"I want it now. I can't wait. Tomorrow doesn't matter."

Reality Check: Recovery requires patience. Your brain needs to relearn how to wait for rewards.

6. Failure to Consider Consequences

"I didn't think about what would happen. I just acted."

Reality Check: Every action has consequences. Practice the "pause" before decisions.

7. Lack of Empathy

"I don't care how they feel. That's their problem, not mine."

Reality Check: Empathy connects you to humanity and prevents harm. Your victims were real people.

Self-Check: Which patterns do you recognize in yourself?

Honest self-assessment is crucial. Take time to identify which thinking errors you've used to justify addiction or criminal behavior. Write about them in your journal.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) Approach

CBT teaches you to identify, challenge, and change distorted thinking:

The ABC Model

  • A - Activating Event (what happened)
  • B - Belief (what you told yourself about it)
  • C - Consequence (how you felt and what you did)

Example:

A: Someone disrespected me in the yard
B: "I can't let anyone disrespect me. I have to respond with violence."
C: I got in a fight and lost privileges.

Challenge the belief: "Is that really true? What would strength and self-respect actually look like? Walking away takes more courage than violence."

Building Pro-Social Thinking

Replace criminal thinking with recovery thinking:

  • Accountability: "I am responsible for my choices and their consequences."
  • Empathy: "My actions affect others. I will consider how my choices impact people."
  • Delayed Gratification: "I can wait. Long-term goals matter more than instant pleasure."
  • Problem-Solving: "I will think through solutions instead of reacting impulsively."
  • Respect: "I will treat others as I want to be treated, even when it's hard."
